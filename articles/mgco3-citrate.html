Additive impact on early-stage magnesium carbonate mineralisation

Year: 2024 Journal: Geochemical Perspectives Letters, 32, 46–51

TEM image of amorphous magnesium carbonate nanoparticles cross-linked by Mg²⁺–citrate bridges.

Why this research matters

Global warming has pushed scientists to look for ways to trap atmospheric CO₂ permanently inside minerals — a strategy called mineral carbonation. Magnesium carbonate (MgCO₃) is one of the most appealing hosts because magnesium-rich silicates are abundant on Earth's surface. There is a catch, though: the thermodynamically stable form, magnesite, stubbornly refuses to crystallise at ambient temperature. Instead, the system produces a parade of hydrated phases — nesquehonite (MgCO₃·3H₂O), dypingite, hydromagnesite — that carry extra water, are bulkier, and dissolve more easily. None of that is ideal for long-term carbon storage.

Understanding and controlling the very first moments of MgCO₃ formation — the prenucleation stage where dissolved ions begin clustering before any solid appears — is therefore critical. Additives can steer this process, but how exactly they act has remained surprisingly unclear.

The question: How does a simple, low-molecular-weight additive like sodium citrate alter the earliest steps of magnesium carbonate precipitation — and can it push the system toward less hydrated, more stable phases better suited for permanent CO₂ storage?

What we did

We carried out constant-pH titration experiments, slowly adding Mg²⁺ to a carbonate buffer while monitoring three signals in real time: solution transmittance (which drops when particles form), electrical conductivity (which reveals how many ions are being consumed by clustering), and particle size via in situ dynamic light scattering (DLS). Citrate was added at concentrations ranging from 0.04 to 5 mM. Solid precipitates were then characterised ex situ by TEM, FESEM, XRD, FTIR and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA/DSC).

Key findings

1. Citrate delays nucleation by destabilising prenucleation clusters

Conductivity tracking revealed that, before any solid appears, Mg²⁺ and CO₃²⁻ ions form solution associates — aggregates larger than simple ion pairs, consistent with what the literature calls prenucleation clusters (PNCs). Citrate disrupted this clustering. The mechanism is entropic: ion association is primarily driven by the entropy gain from releasing coordination water around Mg²⁺. Because citrate promotes Mg²⁺ dehydration (as shown by atomistic simulations), there is less water to release when Mg²⁺ and CO₃²⁻ associate, making clustering thermodynamically less favourable. The result is a measurable, dose-dependent delay in the onset of AMC (amorphous magnesium carbonate) nucleation.

2. The system is forced through liquid–liquid separation

Because citrate hampers the normal clustering pathway, the system overshoots the spinodal limit and undergoes liquid–liquid phase separation before solid AMC forms. TEM images of quenched aliquots captured the fingerprint of this process: shapeless, emulsion-like structures of spherical nanoparticles connected by neck-like bridges — a morphology characteristic of spinodal decomposition that was absent in additive-free controls.

3. Less water in the final product — better for carbon storage

TGA showed that control AMC contained about 1.82 moles of water per mole of MgCO₃. Citrate at low concentration reduced this value. After ageing in the mother liquor for one week, control AMC recrystallised into nesquehonite (MgCO₃·3H₂O), whereas citrate-bearing AMC transformed into dypingite (Mg₅(CO₃)₄(OH)₂·5H₂O) — a less hydrated phase with lower solubility and higher density per mole of stored CO₂. DSC confirmed that citrate-bearing AMC decomposed at a temperature about 100 °C higher than the control, indicating greater thermal stability.

🌍 Why does hydration matter for CO₂ storage?
Less hydrated Mg-carbonate phases pack more CO₂ per unit mass and volume, resist dissolution better, and remain stable over geological timescales. Shifting the crystallisation product from nesquehonite to dypingite — using nothing more than traces of a food-grade additive — could meaningfully improve the economics of mineral carbonation.

4. Citrate also controls nanoparticle aggregation

DLS revealed that citrate initially produced smaller clusters (~2 nm) that rapidly grew to ~40 nm via liquid-phase coalescence, then shrank back to 5–10 nm as AMC nucleated and dehydrated. At longer times, µm-sized aggregates appeared — not seen in controls. We propose that citrate binds to AMC nanoparticle surfaces (analogous to citrate-stabilised gold nanoparticles), initially preventing aggregation through steric repulsion but eventually cross-linking particles via Mg²⁺ complexation of citrate chains.

The bigger picture

This work shows that a cheap, non-toxic, food-grade additive — citric acid's sodium salt — can replicate the effects of far more complex polymers on mineral nucleation. The mechanism is rooted in something fundamental: the entropy of hydration water. By partially dehydrating Mg²⁺ before it even begins to cluster, citrate reshapes the entire crystallisation pathway, from prenucleation associates through liquid–liquid separation to a less hydrated, more durable final solid. The implications reach beyond carbon capture: the same principle applies wherever cation dehydration is the rate-limiting step in mineralisation — from biomineralisation (bone, shell) to the carbonation of historic dolomitic lime mortars and plasters.
