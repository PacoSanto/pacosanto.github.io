Photocatalytic Performance and Carbonation of TiO₂ and ZnO-Modified Sustainable Lime Renders

Year: 2025 (submitted) Journal: Manuscript in preparation

SEM comparison: extensive shrinkage cracks in reference lime mortar (left) vs crack-free ZnO-modified mortar (right) after 5 months curing.

Why this research matters

Buildings consume about 40 % of global energy and generate 36 % of CO₂ emissions during operation. On top of that, keeping facades clean — scrubbing soot, biological growth, and pollutant stains — adds 15–25 % to lifecycle costs and generates its own waste stream of chemical runoff. Self-cleaning surfaces that decompose organic contaminants under sunlight could slash maintenance while cutting environmental impact. Titanium dioxide (TiO₂) has been the default photocatalyst for decades, but its production is energy-intensive (165 MJ/kg) and carbon-heavy (5.0 kg CO₂ eq./kg). Could zinc oxide (ZnO), made via a far simpler metallurgical process, do the same job more sustainably?

Adding photocatalytic nanoparticles to lime-based renders (rather than cement) makes the proposition even more attractive: lime calcination requires only 900 °C (vs 1 450 °C for cement), and the hardened mortar reabsorbs atmospheric CO₂ through carbonation — potentially sequestering up to 40 % of its production emissions over its lifetime.

The question: Can ZnO nanoparticles mixed into lime renders match TiO₂'s photocatalytic self-cleaning performance while preventing drying cracks, preserving carbonation, and cutting the carbon footprint of the photocatalyst itself by more than 80 %?

What we did

We prepared lime plasters (pure binder) and mortars (binder + sand) with 1, 3 and 5 wt% TiO₂ or ZnO relative to binder. After 5 months of natural carbonation at 20 °C and 65 % RH, we evaluated them across six axes:

1

Photocatalytic self-cleaning

Surface staining with methylene blue and rhodamine B applied via a novel marker-based method, then degradation under UV-A (250 W/m², 90 h) tracked by CIE L*a*b* colorimetry.

2

Microstructure & crack analysis

SEM imaging of carbonated surfaces; mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP) for pore-size distribution and total porosity.

3

Carbonation kinetics

TGA at 11 time-points over 160 days; fitting with Avrami-Erofeev, diffusion, and reaction-order models.

4

Laser speckle imaging

Real-time, non-invasive monitoring of drying dynamics and particle rearrangement in fresh mortar drops — capturing the moment cracks form (or don't).

5

Life cycle assessment

Cradle-to-gate LCA comparing TiO₂ (sulfate process) vs ZnO (indirect French process) across 18 impact categories (ReCiPe 2016).

Key findings

1. ZnO eliminates drying cracks; TiO₂ only reduces them

SEM revealed that reference renders are riddled with shrinkage cracks extending hundreds of micrometres. ZnO-modified mortars, by contrast, displayed a uniformly compact microstructure with no cracks at all. TiO₂ achieved only partial crack suppression. MIP explained the mechanism: ZnO selectively eliminates the ~10 µm pore population that initiates cracking, while preserving the smaller capillary pores essential for vapour transport and continued carbonation. In plasters (no aggregate), ZnO increased porosity to 53 % while keeping the pore-size distribution monomodal — a counterintuitive combination that distributes capillary stresses uniformly, preventing the differential strain that triggers cracks.

ZnO's near-neutral surface at the carbonated lime pH (~8.5) allows closer approach of the zwitterionic rhodamine B molecule, enhancing adsorption and degradation. For the cationic methylene blue, both photocatalysts performed comparably. The colorimetric trajectory in the a*–b* plane showed that MB degrades along the blue → neutral axis, while RhB follows a curved path reflecting sequential N-deethylation intermediates — a fingerprint of the oxidative mechanism.

3. Carbonation preserved: 70–82 % at 160 days

The Avrami-Erofeev nucleation-and-growth model fitted all compositions best (R² > 0.85–0.99). Reference mortar reached 82 % carbonation; TiO₂-modified mortars 74–80 %; ZnO-modified 70–72 %. The slight reduction with ZnO correlates with its pore refinement: fewer large pores means higher tortuosity and slower CO₂ diffusion. For a typical 15–20 mm render, this translates to full carbonation in 8–10 months instead of 6–8 — well within normal construction schedules.

🔬 Laser speckle imaging: watching cracks not form
A Nd:YAG 532 nm laser illuminated drying mortar drops while a CMOS camera recorded speckle pattern fluctuations over 48 hours. Reference samples showed three phases: rapid evaporation → particle migration → crack formation. In photocatalyst-modified samples, the crack phase simply never appeared. ZnO mortars showed the most uniform evaporation profiles, suggesting enhanced particle stability in the pore solution.

The indirect French process (zinc vaporisation at 910 °C + air oxidation) is vastly simpler than the sulfate-route synthesis of anatase TiO₂, which involves concentrated H₂SO₄ digestion, multiple purification steps, and high-temperature calcination. Sensitivity analysis confirmed that even under worst-case assumptions for ZnO (coal-heavy grid) and best-case for TiO₂ (renewables-heavy grid), ZnO retains at least 60 % lower emissions.

The bigger picture

This study makes the case that ZnO deserves serious consideration as the photocatalytic additive of choice for sustainable lime-based construction. It matches or exceeds TiO₂ in photocatalytic performance (depending on contaminant type), dramatically outperforms it in crack prevention, and carries a fraction of the environmental cost. The crack-prevention benefit alone — achieved through pore-structure refinement and heterogeneous nucleation of CaCO₃ on nanoparticle surfaces — could extend render service life by years, reducing the cycle of demolition, disposal and reconstruction that adds hidden carbon to a building's lifetime. Future work will explore hybrid TiO₂–ZnO systems to exploit their complementary selectivities, and field trials to validate long-term performance under real weathering.
